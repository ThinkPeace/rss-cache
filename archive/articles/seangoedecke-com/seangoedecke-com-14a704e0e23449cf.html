It doesn’t seem like Luddism is a good model for anti-AI

  • luddites were drawn from a specific job (weavers) that was reasonably high-status and then went away overnight, not an amorphous “life is getting slightly worse because of this new technology” group

    • that meant that luddites had really specific goals & asks (higher piecework wages, a phased introduction of specific machines) that were more appealing than just “stop it all”
  • luddism was also really local. people would gather in the tavern that was in the same town as the mill, then get mad together and all go down to the mill to smash the machines
  • luddism had almost wall-to-wall support in its social class. nobody ratted for years despite huge bribes (and even then it was mostly people arrested in raids who flipped). anti AI sentiment is in the majority but not this universal (yet?)

  • the target of luddism was specific, expensive (Q: how expensive to make) machines that could be straightforwardly sabotaged

    • what’s the tech equivalent? the datacenter? that doesn’t prevent the models from being served or trained elsewhere (at slightly higher latency). The assets themselves (GPUs & hardware) are expensive and fragile, but the actual product displacing labor (the model) is just information. People can run models locally, or stand up home DCs at comparatively cheap cost. People do it for their own solopreneur stuff!
    • victory could be local. if you kept looms out of your town, that temporarily saved industry (still dying).

  • and even then THE LUDDITES LOST. They got soundly crushed by the state

    • there was some support in government for them but not enough
    • it was a big state effort, but states exert a big effort to protect their hugely productive new technologies! that’s one lesson from the story of the luddites
    • Textiles machinery was maybe slightly delayed but continued accelerating and has now won
    • …although a huge, wasteful, bloody, ultimately failed revolution is still a compelling rhetorical technique to compel better laws, I suppose

Is there an example of anti-technology protest that actually worked? Nuclear power, probably? But that was emphatically not grassroots, that was one huge moneyed class of influence going up against another, with the backdrop of the nuclear bomb as a one-off persuasive technique. And it was probably a terrible idea.

From my perspective it reads like a shocking absence of government. Previously I guess the industry was so close (everyone knew everyone very well) and expertise-dependent that people could just work it out together. When that changed - when it became possible for the owners of equipment to “win” - there was yet no legal framework for the workers to use (unions were illegal, there was no democracy, and there was zero appetite for workplace laws, even a symbolic attempt). So it was just workers trying to bully factory-owners, and factory-owners trying to brutalize workers, back and forth forever.

“Today, when on-demand apps are degrading working conditions, AI services are deployed to erode wages, and industrial automation is pushed further into assembly lines and warehouse spaces, workers might remember how close the Luddites came to repelling the technologies of their oppression. They might remember what worked—tight-knit solidarity, distributed organizing models, shows of power and creativity capable of inspiring influential producers of culture, unrepentantly aggressive actions against those oppressive technologies specifically; and what did not—violence against individuals, a lack of coordination with an empowered political body, the absence of a sustained effort to grow one. They might recognize that the Luddites failed in the face of totalizing authoritarianism, and that such conditions are not static. We can still oppose the creep of totalizing control into our lives. We can look at certain technologies, certain modes of domination, and say: No.” p244

I don’t know about this. Violence against individuals seemed like it worked well enough. Even before the murder of Horsfall, Luddites were firing blindly into buildings and setting them on fire, and the murder of Horsfall did cause his heirs to remove the machinery from his factory. Plausibly the Luddites showed a lot of restraint, which did help. But I do wonder if Merchant here is going to some trouble to reach his desired “don’t be terrorists” conclusion

A total failure of leadership

Reading through the series of events from a modern perspective, I was struck by the near-total absence of government. The artisans were left to work out their grievances with their bosses more or less by themselves, with no formal channels for complaint or any attempt at mediation. When the government did intervene - in response to near-universal unrest in half of the country - they did this:

  1. Make machine-breaking and oath-taking capital crimes
  2. Dump thousands of soldiers more or less at random into the area, with no plan to guard factories or do anything beyond just hang around in case a revolution broke out
  3. Empower a single magistrate to arrest and interrogate whoever he wanted in order to root out the conspiracy

I suppose it worked, in the sense that it eventually succeeded in stopping the Luddist raids. But I can’t help but think that even a token gesture of conciliation would have gone a long way towards calming things down. This almost actually happened! There were various pro-worker platforms of legislation being proposed at the time, but they didn’t have enough support to pass and the Prince Regent (who comes off very badly in Blood in the Machine) had no interest in the topic.

Prior to the 1800s, I suppose the workers and bosses of the English textiles industry were a small enough group (and the bosses were sufficiently dependent on the workers’ expertise) to be able to just work out their problems together. When that changed - when it became possible for the bosses to “win” - there wasn’t yet any legal framework for the workers to use. Labor unions were illegal, there was no democracy, and the government wasn’t willing or able to mediate. So it was just workers trying to bully factory-owners (via riots and death threats), and factory-owners trying to brutalize workers, back and forth forever.
