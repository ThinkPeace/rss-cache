The function

(1 + cos(x))/2

gives a fair approximation to the Gaussian density

exp(−x²)

You can make the approximation much better by raising it to a power. The function

((1 + cos(x))/2)4

gives a good lower bound and

((1 + cos(x))/2)3.5597

gives a good upper bound. More on that here.

There are other ways of improving the cosine approximation to the Gaussian. Yesterday I came across one I hadn’t seen before, adding a sin(x) term to x.

(1 + cos(sin(x) + x))/2

This function matches the first few terms of the power series for exp(−x²) and has an error on the order of x6/240. You can’t see the difference between the two functions in a plot for −4 ≤ x ≤ 4.

There’s some tension between the previous two statements. If the error in on the order of x6/240 then we’d expect the error to be huge at x = 4. We have an alternating series, so the truncation error should be roughly equal to the first term after the truncation, right? No, the alternating series theorem doesn’t apply because the absolute values of the terms in the series are not decreasing yet. They have to decrease eventually because the series has infinite radius of convergence, but they’re not decreasing at the 6th term.
